The Big Three at Paris — Aims and Demands
8 mins
▼

Read the table carefully. Then cover it and complete the recall task below without looking back.

Leader Country Key Aims Why
Woodrow Wilson USA 14 Points: self-determination, no punishing peace, League of Nations, arms reduction, freedom of the seas USA entered late; Wilson believed a fair peace would prevent future war. He was an idealist.
David Lloyd George Britain Punish Germany but not too harshly. Keep German trade alive. Protect British naval supremacy. Gain German colonies. British public wanted revenge ("Hang the Kaiser"), but Lloyd George feared a crushed Germany would turn communist or seek revenge.
Georges Clemenceau France Crush Germany permanently. Huge reparations. Demilitarised Rhineland. Return of Alsace-Lorraine. Reduce Germany's army to near zero. France had been invaded twice in 50 years. Clemenceau represented a traumatised nation that wanted security above all else.
Key conflict between the three: Wilson wanted a fair peace; Clemenceau wanted Germany destroyed; Lloyd George sat in the middle. This tension shaped every term of the Treaty of Versailles.

Treaty of Versailles — Key Terms
7 mins
▼

These are the terms the examiner expects you to know precisely. Use dates and figures, not vague descriptions.

Area Term Why Germany hated it
War Guilt Clause 231: Germany had to accept full blame for WWI Germans saw this as a lie. They believed Austria-Hungary and Russia were equally to blame.
Reparations £6.6 billion (fixed 1921). Payable over decades. Crippled the German economy. Led to hyperinflation by 1923.
Military Army limited to 100,000 men. No air force. Navy limited to 6 battleships. Rhineland demilitarised. Germany felt humiliated and defenceless.
Territory Lost Alsace-Lorraine (to France), Posen and West Prussia (to Poland), all overseas colonies, Saarland under League control for 15 years. Lost 10% of land and 12.5% of population. 3 million Germans in Poland, under foreign rule.
League of Nations Germany not invited to join at first. League would enforce the treaty. Germany excluded from the body meant to maintain world peace.

League of Nations — Aims, Structure & Weaknesses
8 mins
▼
Aspect Detail
Founded 1920. Based in Geneva, Switzerland.
Aims Maintain world peace through collective security; encourage disarmament; improve living and working conditions; enforce the Treaty of Versailles.
Assembly All member nations. Met once a year. Unanimous votes required for major decisions.
Council Permanent members: Britain, France, Italy, Japan. Met more frequently. Had power to recommend action.
Permanent Court Settled legal disputes between nations. No enforcement power.

The six structural weaknesses you must know:

  1. USA never joined — the world's largest economy was absent, making sanctions meaningless.
  2. No army — relied on member nations to contribute troops. They rarely did.
  3. Unanimous decisions needed — any single member could block action.
  4. Sanctions ignored — without US trade, targeted nations simply traded elsewhere.
  5. Germany and USSR excluded — weakened legitimacy and representation.
  6. Dominated by Britain and France — both had their own interests and were war-weary.
Recall Task
Name THREE structural weaknesses of the League of Nations.
What were the two main bodies of the League? What was the difference between them?
Why did the absence of the USA make economic sanctions almost useless?
Any three from: no USA; no army; unanimous decisions required; sanctions ignored; Germany/USSR excluded; dominated by Britain and France with their own interests.
The Assembly (all members, met annually) and the Council (permanent members, met more often and could recommend action). The Council had more decision-making power.
Any country hit by sanctions could simply trade with the USA instead. US businesses had no obligation to stop trading, so the sanctions had huge gaps from the start.
4
League in Action — Key Crises and Successes
7 mins
▼
Common error to fix now: The Manchurian Crisis (1931) is outside the 1920s timeframe. If an exam question asks about the League "in the 1920s", use Corfu (1923) and Upper Silesia (1921) as your failure examples, not Manchuria.
Event Date What happened Result Success or failure?
Aaland Islands 1921 Finland and Sweden both claimed the islands. League ruled in Finland's favour. Sweden accepted the ruling peacefully. SUCCESS
Upper Silesia 1921 Germany and Poland both wanted the industrial region. League held a vote, then divided the territory. Poland got the industrial areas; Germany got more land. Both sides unhappy. The messy compromise created resentment. PARTIAL FAILURE
Corfu Crisis 1923 Mussolini bombed and occupied the Greek island of Corfu after an Italian general was killed. Greece appealed to the League. League condemned Italy. Italy threatened to leave. League backed down. Italy collected a fine from Greece directly. The bully won. FAILURE
Greek–Bulgarian 1925 Greece invaded Bulgaria. League condemned Greece and ordered withdrawal. Greece obeyed. A genuine success. SUCCESS
Manchurian Crisis 1931 Japan invaded Manchuria in China. League condemned Japan, set up the Lytton Commission. Japan ignored it and left the League. No sanctions. Japan kept Manchuria. League shown to be powerless against a major power. FAILURE

The pattern to spot (essential for Level 4): The League succeeded when no major power had something to gain. It failed whenever a major power chose to ignore it. This is not a coincidence — it reflects the structural weakness that the League had no army and no real enforcement power.

Recall Task
Name two genuine League successes in the 1920s.
Why was the Corfu Crisis (1923) a serious failure for the League?
What pattern explains why the League succeeded in some cases but failed in others?
Aaland Islands (1921): Finland vs Sweden, resolved peacefully. Greek-Bulgarian dispute (1925): League ordered Greece out of Bulgaria and Greece obeyed. Also: humanitarian work (freeing 200,000 slaves in Sierra Leone; founding what became the WHO; repatriating WWI prisoners).
Italy — a major power and a founding League member — broke international law. The League condemned Italy, but when Italy threatened to leave, the League backed down and let Italy collect a fine from Greece. The aggressor won. This sent a message to other nations that the League would not confront powerful countries.
The League succeeded only when no major power had a strong national interest at stake. When minor nations clashed with no powerful patron, the League could resolve it. When a major power (Italy in Corfu, Japan in Manchuria) had something to gain, it simply ignored the League, and the League had nothing to back up its condemnation.

Short-Answer Drill — 4-mark Questions
8 mins
▼

These questions are worth 4 marks each. Aim for: one specific named example, one explanation of what it shows, one link to the broader problem. Do not bullet-point — write in sentences.

Question A — 4 marks — Allow 3 minutes
Describe one way the Treaty of Versailles caused resentment in Germany.

The War Guilt Clause (Article 231) forced Germany to accept full responsibility for causing World War One. Germans deeply resented this because they believed Austria-Hungary had been equally responsible for starting the war. The clause was used to justify the enormous reparations bill of £6.6 billion, which crippled the German economy and generated lasting anger that nationalist politicians, including Hitler, later exploited.

Marker note: award credit for naming the specific clause or figure, and for explaining why Germany considered it unjust. A bare statement without explanation scores 1–2 marks; explanation with evidence scores 3–4.

Question B — 4 marks — Allow 3 minutes
Describe one reason why the League of Nations struggled to enforce its decisions.

The League had no army of its own. It relied entirely on member nations to contribute troops, but they were rarely willing to do so after the losses of World War One. This meant that whenever the League condemned a nation's aggression, it had nothing to back up its words. The Corfu Crisis of 1923 showed this clearly: Italy simply ignored the League's condemnation, and the League had no military force to compel compliance.

Grade 5 vs Grade 9 — What "Sustained Judgement" Actually Means
5 mins
▼

Read both versions of the same paragraph. Notice what the Grade 9 version does that the Grade 5 version does not.

Grade 5 — Describes but does not judge

The Corfu Crisis of 1923 showed the League failing. Italy invaded Corfu after an Italian general was killed. The League condemned Italy but Italy threatened to leave, so the League backed down. This shows that the League failed when major powers were involved.

Grade 9 — Explains, links, judges

The Corfu Crisis of 1923 provides the strongest evidence that the League failed more than it succeeded when major powers were involved. Italy — a founding member — bombed and occupied Corfu, was condemned by the League, and then simply refused to comply, threatening to leave. The League backed down. This links directly to the structural weakness identified above: without an army or US economic support, the League had nothing to back up its condemnation. This therefore supports the statement, because it shows that the League's authority was conditional on the agreement of those with power — and the moment a major power chose to resist, the League was helpless.

The three moves that make the Grade 9 version work:

  1. Names the structural weakness it links back to (no army, no USA).
  2. Explains the significance, not just the event.
  3. Ends by directly answering "how far" — this is the "sustained judgement" the mark scheme requires.

Grade 9 Model Answer — with annotations

I largely agree that the League of Nations failed more often than it succeeded in the 1920s, primarily because its structural weaknesses made it unable to enforce its decisions against determined aggressors. INTRO: clear position + two named reasons previewed Although the League achieved genuine successes in resolving minor disputes and delivering important humanitarian work, these were only possible because no major power had a strong competing interest at stake. POSITION

The most important reason the League failed more than it succeeded was its fundamental structural weakness: the refusal of the United States to join. POINT: names the specific weakness Although the League had been proposed by American President Woodrow Wilson as part of his Fourteen Points, the US Congress voted against membership in 1920, fearing entanglement in European affairs. EVIDENCE Without the world's largest economy, the League had neither the economic leverage to make sanctions effective nor the military credibility to deter aggressors. Any nation targeted by sanctions knew that the USA would continue to trade with them regardless. EXPLAIN This structural weakness meant the League was destined to fail whenever it faced a determined aggressor with something to gain, because it simply lacked the power to compel obedience. JUDGEMENT

The Corfu Crisis of 1923 provides the clearest evidence that the League failed when major powers were involved. Following the murder of an Italian general on the Greek-Albanian border, Mussolini ordered the bombardment and occupation of the Greek island of Corfu. EVIDENCE The League condemned Italy's action, but when Italy threatened to leave the organisation, the League backed down and allowed Italy to collect a financial penalty from Greece directly. This was a significant failure because the aggressor — a founding member of the League — had broken international law, faced condemnation, and then refused to comply. This links directly to the structural weakness: without an army and without the USA's economic weight, the League had nothing to back up its condemnation. EXPLAIN + LINK The Corfu Crisis sent a damaging message to other nations that aggression against smaller countries would go largely unpunished. JUDGEMENT

The League also failed to resolve several territorial disputes in the 1920s. In 1920, Poland seized the Lithuanian capital of Vilna by force. Lithuania appealed to the League, but the League took no meaningful action, partly because France and Britain were reluctant to confront Poland, which they viewed as a buffer state against communist Russia. EVIDENCE Similarly, the Upper Silesia crisis of 1921 showed the League struggling even when it did act: the League organised a vote and then divided the region, giving Poland the more valuable industrial areas while Germany received more land. Both sides were left dissatisfied. EXPLAIN These cases show that the League failed not only through inaction but also through ineffective action, suggesting its failures ran deeper than a simple lack of willpower. JUDGEMENT

However, it would be wrong to say the League never succeeded. In 1921, the League successfully resolved the Aaland Islands dispute between Finland and Sweden. In 1925, it ordered Greece to withdraw from Bulgaria and Greece obeyed. The League's humanitarian agencies also produced real, lasting results: freeing over 200,000 slaves in Sierra Leone and founding what would later become the World Health Organisation. EVIDENCE These were genuine achievements. However, they all share one crucial characteristic: they occurred in situations where no major power had a strong national interest at stake. By contrast, when Italy had something to gain in Corfu, it simply ignored the League. This reveals the fundamental limitation of the League's successes — they were conditional on the agreement of those with power. CLEVER HOWEVER

In conclusion, I largely agree with the statement. Its successes — the Aaland Islands, the Greek-Bulgarian dispute, its humanitarian work — were genuine, but they were only possible because they did not require the League to confront a major power with something to lose. Its failures — Corfu, Vilna, Upper Silesia — all shared the same underlying cause: structural weaknesses that left the League unable to enforce its decisions. The absence of the USA was the critical factor, robbing the League of both the economic leverage and the international authority it needed. The League did not simply fail more than it succeeded — it was structurally set up in a way that made decisive success almost impossible whenever the stakes were high enough for a major power to care. DEGREE STATEMENT

What to look for in your own answer:

  • Does every paragraph end with a sentence that answers "how far"?
  • Do you name the structural weakness in every failure paragraph?
  • Does your "However" paragraph identify the pattern in the successes, not just list them?
  • Does your conclusion give a "degree" statement — not just "mainly agree" but WHY the failures outweigh the successes?
  • Have you avoided Manchuria (1931) in a question about the 1920s?
Single Most Important Habit

At the end of every paragraph in a "how far do you agree" question, write one sentence that directly answers the question. Do not describe and move on. After your evidence and explanation, stop and ask yourself: "does this support or challenge the statement, and how significant is it?" Write that answer as your final sentence in the paragraph. This is what "sustained judgement" means in the mark scheme, and it is the only thing that separates Level 3 from Level 4.
