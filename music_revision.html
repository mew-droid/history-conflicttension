Spot the Technique — Read the extract, name the technique
1
The Beatles — "Eleanor Rigby" (1966)
"Eleanor Rigby" uses a string octet throughout. There is no guitar, bass or drums. The strings play chords together in the same rhythm as the vocal melody, moving in block chords. There is no independent instrumental melody running against the voice.
What texture does this describe? Name it precisely.
Homophonic texture (melody and accompaniment / chordal texture)

The strings play chords in the same rhythm as the vocal — this is homophonic texture, specifically chordal homophony. For Grade 8–9, note that this texture directs full attention to the vocal melody, making the stark, isolated lyrics ("died in the church and was buried along with her name") even more affecting. Contrast this with polyphonic texture, where multiple independent lines would compete for the listener's attention.

2
Kanye West — "Gold Digger" (2005)
Throughout "Gold Digger", the same two-bar piano figure from Ray Charles's "I Got a Woman" (1954) repeats continuously underneath Kanye's rapping. The figure never changes; it anchors the entire track from beginning to end.
Name the technique AND the broader compositional device this figure represents.
Ostinato (also: the track uses sampling)

An ostinato is a short melodic or rhythmic pattern that repeats continuously throughout a piece or section. The piano loop is a melodic ostinato. For full marks, you should also mention that Kanye uses sampling — incorporating a pre-recorded extract from another track. Sampling is a key compositional technique in hip-hop. In the exam, if asked about texture, the repeated piano creates a homophonic foundation with the rap melody on top.

3
J.S. Bach — Badinerie (Set Work)
In bars 1–4 of the Badinerie, the flute plays a four-note descending motif. In bars 5–8, the same four-note shape appears again, but starting two steps lower. The shape of the figure is identical; only the pitch level changes.
Identify the melodic device used and explain what makes it distinctive.
Sequence (melodic sequence)

A sequence is when a melodic idea is repeated at a different pitch level, preserving the same intervals and rhythmic shape. This is one of the most important devices in Baroque music. Bach uses sequences to develop short motifs rather than constantly introducing new material — it gives the piece forward momentum while keeping it compact. If the sequence moves downward in pitch, it is a descending sequence; upward is an ascending sequence.

4
The Beatles — "Here Comes the Sun" (1969)
At the end of the chorus in "Here Comes the Sun", the harmony moves from chord IV to chord I. This progression has a calm, devotional quality and is associated with the word "Amen" in church music.
Name the cadence and describe what it sounds like compared to a perfect cadence.
Plagal cadence (IV–I)

A plagal cadence moves from chord IV to chord I. It sounds softer and more gentle than a perfect cadence (V–I). The perfect cadence has a strong sense of resolution and finality; the plagal cadence feels more peaceful and understated. In an exam, always give the chord numerals (IV–I) as well as the name. The Beatles used plagal cadences frequently — it gives their music a hymn-like quality. This is also called the "Amen cadence".

5
Toto — "Africa" (Set Work)
In the introduction to "Africa", the keyboard plays a repeated rhythmic and melodic figure while the drums provide a separate rhythm beneath it. The accents in the keyboard part do not fall on the main beats of the bar; instead they fall between the main beats, creating a sense of rhythmic tension and forward motion.
Name the rhythmic device and explain why it creates tension.
Syncopation

Syncopation is the displacement of the regular beat, so that accents fall on normally weak beats or between main beats. It creates tension because the listener's ear anticipates the main beat but the accent arrives somewhere else, creating rhythmic friction. In "Africa", the syncopated keyboard intro is a melodic ostinato with syncopated rhythm — two techniques in one. In the exam, always explain the effect: syncopation creates a sense of momentum and rhythmic interest, common in pop, jazz, and African-influenced music.

6
Trumpet technique — identify the extended technique
A trumpet player rapidly alternates their tongue between a "t" and "r" sound while blowing through the instrument, producing a rapid, fluttering tone quality rather than a clear, sustained note. This was used extensively by jazz trumpeters and appears in some contemporary orchestral writing.
Name the technique and name one other extended technique used by brass players.
Flutter tonguing (flutter-tongue)

Flutter tonguing creates a rapid, buzzing quality by rolling or fluttering the tongue while playing. Other brass extended techniques include: muting (inserting a straight, cup, or harmon mute into the bell to change timbre — written "con sordino"); half-valving (depressing valves halfway to produce a distorted pitch); multiphonics (producing more than one pitch simultaneously). Timbre questions in the exam want specific technical vocabulary, not vague descriptions like "breathy" or "buzzy".
